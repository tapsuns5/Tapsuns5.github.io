Let me start off by saying that for years, I have told myself, "I will learn how to code," or I kept telling myself and my wife, "I want to learn how to code." The only thing stopping me was myself. Browsing around, I found the #100DaysofCodeChallenge on Twitter (now X) and began my journey. All I was missing was public accountability and a small commitment per day to learning with no pressure. Here are my biggest takeaways from this experience that I want to share with you.

1. Public Accountability

For me, this was the start I needed. Find a community. Find people on the same journey as you and connect with others. There is something about seeing people trying the same things you are doing. You don't feel as alone when you are going through the struggles especially if it is your first time jumping into code. You are most likely on this journey at the same time with other people who work a full time job just like yourself.

2. Commit to what you can, but commit

I believe one of the rules of this challenge is to code for literally 100 days straight. I think I did this for about 50 days until it wasn't really feasbile anymore. 50 days was quite the accomplishment thought. My next 50 days took a lot longer than the first, but I am still here writing to say I completed my 100 days. Life happens and you can't always control things, but what is important is finishing your committment.

3. Progress Over Perfection

I started with Codecademy's full stack course which began with HTML, CSS, and then moved to vanilla Javascript. It wasn't until more advanced Javascript concepts did I start to hit some roadblocks. It may have been because of the way I learn (everyone has their own ways) but I struggled to retain some of the concepts before moving into React. The React course then became a blur. Then the backend course became a blur once it jumped from NodeJS to ExpressJS. The most important thing was to still keep moving forward and when things became difficult, I took a step back and did more reading than actually trying to code. This will lead me to the next section.

4. Projects During Progress

If you look at my twitter posts, I made the most progress probably the past 2-3 weeks. I was taking things a bit too slow the last 25 days when I actually had a pretty solid understanding of concepts and could start to mess around. I shipped things to GitHub and learned how to create my bio here. I shipped a full stack application with PHP. I also created my first full stack JS application with a MERN stack (MongoDB, ExpressJS, ReactJS, NodeJS). It obviously took a lot of google searching and working with ChatGPT but that is part fo the journey.

5. End Goal in Mind

I firmly believe that learning how to code and build software is once fo the ways to not only create value for yourself in the workplace but that it is a massive opportuntiy for you to create financial freedom with your own business. Sure, you could hire someone to build your product but not everyone has the capital to do so. Learning to code opens so many opportunities for you. Which is why this topic is to have that end goal in mind. What do you want to build? What problem do you want to solve?

6. Search and Chat

I have been in tech for the last 9 years and in Product the last 4. I am very accustomed to tech and the concepts. I never could really grasp though the engineering and devloper memes because I ahve never actually been in their shoes. Well, now I truly understand all the stackoverflow memes and the google searching for code memes. It really is just part of the craft. You understanding the concepts, you have the mindeset, but syntax always evolves and you often work with many different languages. It takes 10,000 hours in order to master a skill. Now, multiply that by the many langues you will need to know and the ever evolving nature of syntax in coding. It is OKAY to google search and use AI engines to help with your code. It is those that don't know how to use it that will get passed up. Just make sure you understand the concepts and what that code is doing! It helps to rewrite it yourself in your own code in a different feature in order to retain the information.

7. 🚢 Ship Ship Ship 🚢

You don't really learn anything about buildig software until you ship your first website. It could just be a page that says Hello World. Learning to code is much more that just writing the code which is why this is title Ship!🚢 Learning git, local development, file structure, .env and .gitignore files for dev to prod deployments, handling local development with database, remote databases and remote servers in general, and not to mention packages and their dependeancies. I would reccomend anyone to try Codecademy and their course. I thought it was decent. FreeCodeCamp is free and is also great. But nothing replaces shipping things and getting your hands dirty to just FIO! (figure it out).
